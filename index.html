Research Background

Purpose

  • Support product and GTM with packaged insights on how boards measure and use governance insights today.
  • Understand AI interest, expectations, and trust requirements for governance insights.

Distribution

  • Survey distributed to board leaders (directors, CEOs, executive directors, corporate secretaries, board administrators).
  • Focus on roles, industries, and current vs. future maturity.

Response

  • 358 surveys completed with key questions answered.
  • How participants access insights today is highly varied (board portals, spreadsheets, email, informal methods)—no single dominant tool.
  • Analysis includes cross-tabs by role and industry; segment callouts noted in key findings.

Methodology & limitations

  • Self-reported responses; single wave. No prior wave for year-over-year comparison.
  • Participants who completed key questions (maturity, AI interest, or related) are included in N=358.
  • For best experience and charts, view in a modern browser; use Print to PDF if a static copy is needed.

Executive Summary

Board effectiveness and governance insights maturity are mixed; a majority expect to be in “Developing” or “Advanced” in 12 months. AI interest is strong, with trust and controls as prerequisites.

  • Board insights maturity is spread across Developing (33%), Basic (27%), and Advanced (25%). 78% expect to be in Developing or Advanced in 12 months.
  • 72% of participants are somewhat to very interested (3–5) in using AI-generated governance insights; 8% are already using AI today.
  • Top barrier to insights: “Exists but fragmented/manual” (55% of respondents selected)—followed by “No owner/follow-through” and “Not actionable.”
  • Strong data privacy, human review before sharing, and transparent audit trails are the top requirements for AI-generated insights.
  • Ease of accessing insights varies by role: Corporate Secretary/GC report highest ease; Executive Directors report lowest on director engagement.

Key Findings

How Boards Measure and Use Governance Insights Today

Which statement best describes how your board currently measures and uses governance insights? | N=358

Insight: One-third are “Developing” (periodic reviews, insights not always timely or acted on). Product can lean into structured reporting and follow-through to meet this segment.

Where Boards Expect to Be in 12 Months

Looking ahead about 12 months, where do you expect your board to be in its approach to measuring and using governance insights? | N=358

Insight: 78% expect “Developing” or “Advanced” in 12 months (vs. 60% today). Advanced-maturity boards are more likely to already use or plan for AI—segment by maturity for GTM.

Interest in AI-Generated Governance Insights

How interested would you be in using AI-generated governance insights to support board oversight and decision-making? (1 = Not interested, 5 = Very interested) | N=358

Segment callout: Corporate Secretary/GC and Board Director/Member show the highest share at 4–5. Financial services and Corporate show relatively higher “very interested” (5)—AI messaging may resonate there first.

Expectation for AI Use in the Next 12 Months

Within the next 12 months, what level of expectation is there that your board or governance team will use AI to generate governance insights? | N=358

Maturity of AI insights: Only 8% “Already using AI” today; 33% have moderate expectation. Meeting trust requirements (privacy, audit trail, human review) can capture the “moderate expectation” segment.

Ease of Accessing Insights by Governance Area

How easy is it to access insights in each of these areas? (% Easy + Very Easy) | N=358

Insight: Director engagement, composition, and strategy are the hardest areas (38–40% easy); meetings run and materials are easiest (56–61%). Product can prioritize visibility into engagement and composition for boards that struggle there.

Barriers to Governance Insights

What gets in the way of boards having the insights they need? …participants allowed to choose more than one | N=358

Product angle: “Fragmented/manual” and “No owner/follow-through” point to workflow and ownership. “Not actionable” and “Hard to locate” point to clarity and discoverability. AI and tooling can target consolidation, clear ownership, and actionable next steps.

Key Distinctions: Insights That Go Beyond the Charts

Cross-question analysis and actionable takeaways—so product and GTM can act on them.

Maturity Predicts AI Adoption

  • Advanced-maturity boards: 21% already using AI today (vs. 5% Informal, 0% Not sure).
  • High expectation (planning/piloting): concentrated in Advanced (9%) and Developing (8%).

Takeaway: Segment by maturity—lead with AI for Advanced/Developing; build trust and basics first for Basic/Informal.

Role Shapes Both Interest and Visibility

  • Corporate Secretary/GC: highest ease—61% Easy+Very Easy on materials, 45% on oversight/risk.
  • Executive Directors: lowest on director engagement (28%).
  • Secretary/GC and Board Director/Member: highest share at AI interest 4–5.

Takeaway: Position “ease of access” for Secretaries; “engagement visibility” and AI for EDs and Directors.

Industry Shifts AI Interest

  • Financial services and Corporate: higher proportion “very interested” (5) than Nonprofit and Education.
  • Nonprofit: largest segment (49%), spread across the interest scale.

Takeaway: Prioritize FinServ and Corporate for AI-first messaging; use trust and workflow for Nonprofit.

One Barrier Dominates—Build There First

  • 55% selected “Exists but fragmented/manual” (195 mentions).
  • No other barrier exceeds 32%.

Takeaway: Lead with consolidation, less manual effort, and “one place” for governance insights.

The “Moderate Expectation” Segment Is the Near-Term Opportunity

  • 33% have moderate expectation (likely within 12 months, no active plan yet).
  • vs. 8% already using and 7% high expectation.

Takeaway: Capture this segment with trust (privacy, audit trail, human review) and clear use cases (follow-through, engagement, materials).

Product Direction: Now / Next / Later

Now

Double down on follow-through and ownership: action tracking, due dates, status. Address “fragmented/manual” and “no owner/follow-through” with clear workflows and visibility.

Next

AI-generated insights with trust first: strong privacy, human review, transparent audit trail, clear citations. Prioritize use cases: director engagement, follow-through, quality of materials.

Later

Structured reporting across governance areas (strategy, risk, composition) and early warning on risks/compliance. Deeper persona-specific views (e.g., engagement for EDs, materials for Secretaries).
